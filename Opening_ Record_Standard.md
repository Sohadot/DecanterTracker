# High-Value Bottle Opening Standard

**Standard ID:** DT-ORS-001
**Maintained by:** DecanterTracker
**Status:** v1 — Public Reference
**Scope:** The opening, service, and consumption of high-value wine, as an accountable event.

---

> **A great bottle is made over years and opened in a moment. Its record should honor both.**
>
> DecanterTracker defines the opening record standard for high-value wine service.

---

## Preamble

Wine systems track what is owned and stored. Cellar software records inventory.
Rating platforms record opinion. Insurance records value. All of them fall silent
at the cork.

This standard governs a different moment: the **irreversible event** of opening a
high-value bottle in service — who opened it, when, before whom, in what condition,
and how it was received.

Opening is not a step in inventory. It is the single moment a bottle stops being an
asset and becomes an experience. It cannot be undone, re-counted, or returned to the
cellar. A bottle made over decades is consumed in an hour, and in most service
contexts no deliberate record of that hour exists.

This standard exists so that the moment is recorded with the same care the bottle
was made with — not to assign blame, not to issue verdicts, not to value. Only to
hold a clear and faithful account of what happened.

---

## The Custody Boundary

This standard defines what the opening record **is** and what it **is not**.
This boundary is binding on every clause below.

**The record attests. It does not adjudicate.**

- It records that a bottle was found corked. It does not rule who is at fault.
- It records that a guest declined a bottle. It does not judge whether they were right.
- It records the condition observed at opening. It does not certify provenance,
  authenticate origin, or assign monetary value.
- It is a faithful account of a service event. It is not a court, an appraiser,
  or an insurer.

Any party may rely on the record as evidence of what was observed. No party should
read the record as a finding, a settlement, or a valuation. The record's authority
comes entirely from its discipline, not from any claim to judgment.

*(This is the Artifact–Subject Separation principle: the record describes the event;
it never performs the verdict on the event.)*

---

## The Seven Clauses

### 1. The Opening Authority

Every opening has an authorizing basis. The record names **who held the authority
to open the bottle**, and under what permission it was exercised.

- The person who opened the bottle (the operator).
- The basis of authority: owner present, owner's standing instruction, service
  order, event protocol, or cellar exit authorization.
- For bottles leaving a private cellar: the authorization to remove the bottle from
  storage, separate from the authorization to open it.

A bottle opened without a recorded basis is a gap in the record, not a neutral
absence. The standard treats unauthorized or unattributed openings as the primary
event the record exists to prevent.

### 2. Pre-Opening Condition Attestation

Before the bottle is touched, its condition is attested. This is the baseline
against which everything after is read.

- Fill level (ullage).
- Capsule and closure condition.
- Label condition and legibility.
- Any provenance reference already on file (lot, purchase, prior storage).
- Visible sediment, seepage, or anomaly.

The attestation is a description, never a grade. It says what was seen. It does not
say whether the bottle is "good."

### 3. The Witness Principle

A high-value opening is rarely private. The record names **before whom** the bottle
was opened.

- Owner, host, or principal present.
- Guest or guests for whom it was served.
- Service team members present at the opening.

The witness is not a formality. In disputed events — a refused bottle, a wrong
bottle, a fault — the question is almost never "was it opened" but "who was present
when it was opened, and what did they observe." The witness clause answers that in
advance.

### 4. The Service Record

The mechanics of the service itself, recorded as they happen.

- Time opened.
- Whether decanted, and when.
- Time poured and served.
- To whom served.
- Temperature at service, if relevant to the bottle.

This is the operational spine of the record. It is also where most wine systems
stop short — they record the bottle, never the pour.

### 5. Fault & Incident Posture

The record's posture toward things going wrong. This clause is the reason the
standard exists in operations, not only in theory.

Incidents recorded include, without limitation:

- Corked (TCA) bottle.
- Oxidized or otherwise faulty bottle.
- Bottle declined or refused by guest or principal.
- Wrong bottle opened.
- Bottle removed from cellar without clear authorization.
- Condition not documented before service.

**The posture is documentary, not judicial.** The record states the incident as
observed. It does not determine cause, assign responsibility, or resolve the
dispute. Its value in a dispute is precisely that it took no side — it only held
the facts.

### 6. Consumption Closure

Every opened bottle reaches a defined end state. The record closes the event
explicitly rather than letting it trail off into memory.

- Consumed.
- Partially consumed (remainder, and its disposition).
- Returned / unfinished (and where it went).
- Lost or unaccounted (recorded as such, not omitted).

An open event with no recorded closure is itself a finding. The standard does not
permit a bottle to simply disappear from the account.

### 7. Record Integrity

The conditions under which a record is trustworthy.

- Recorded at the time of the event, not reconstructed afterward.
- Attributed to a named operator.
- Not editable after closure without a visible, attributed amendment.
- Self-contained: the record means what it says without external explanation.

A record that fails integrity is not invalid — it is simply a weaker account, and
the standard asks that its weakness be visible rather than hidden.

---

## How a record is read

A complete opening record answers, without interpretation:

> **What** bottle, in **what condition**, opened by **whom**, under **what authority**,
> **before whom**, **how served**, with **what incident if any**, reaching **what end**.

Nothing in that sentence is a verdict. Every element is an observation. That is the
whole discipline.

---

## What this standard is not

- It is not insurance documentation, though an insurer may rely on a record.
- It is not provenance authentication, though it references provenance on file.
- It is not a valuation, and contains no monetary judgment.
- It is not inventory or cellar management — it begins where those end.
- It is not a rating, review, or tasting note.

DecanterTracker maintains the standard. The standard does not depend on any single
tool, format, or vendor; it can be expressed on paper, in a sheet, or in software.
What it governs is the discipline of the record, not the medium.
